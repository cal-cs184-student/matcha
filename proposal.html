We're building a Minecraft-style 3D environment in Unity that uses custom shaders to apply textures and non-photorealistic effects inspired by the four seasons of the year. Our goal is to explore how artistic styles can be integrated into real-time rendering using both procedural techniques and shader programming.

Problem Description

Games like Minecraft rely on a simple, iconic visual style: we want to take that foundation and turn it into something more artistically expressive. Our project looks at how to render stylised, themed environments in real time using shader-based techniques. The challenge is to create both visual richness and variety without sacrificing performance or relying on manually designed textures for every object. We’re tackling this by designing shaders that procedurally map 2D textures onto 3D voxel-style assets and by developing non-photorealistic effects that give the world a more handcrafted look. This is especially interesting in the context of real-time graphics because it blends technical rendering pipelines with creative direction, which is something that's not always easy to automate or generalise.

Goals and Deliverables

Part 1: Baseline Plan / What We Plan to Deliver

Our baseline plan is to deliver shader-based mods for Minecraft in Unity’s rendering pipeline, inspired by personalized themes, such as The Lorax and Japan. This will involve developing custom shaders to incorporate unique textures and artistic styles, then applying them to enhance the appearances of both existing Minecraft objects and our own theme-inspired assets (e.g. butterflies, swans, mountain tops).

Texture Shader

Our first objective is to develop a shader that performs texture mapping on assets within a 3D Minecraft-style mesh. For instance, in the Japan-themed demo, we will map 2D patterns of cherry blossoms and cobblestones onto the vanilla 3D models of trees and paths from the Minecraft framework. Specifically, the shader will utilize the approach outlined in the “Solid Texture Synthesis from 2D Exemplars” paper, which will allow us to efficiently generate novel 3D textures from the provided 2D examples.

Style Shader

To render non-photorealistic effects, our goal is to implement a watercolor-like appearance that complements the Japan theme. This shader will apply techniques including soft shading with a light falloff, blurring, global desaturation and noise to simulate the look of smooth, blended watercolor brush strokes on a grainy paper canvas.

Part 2: Aspirational Plan / What We Hope to Deliver

If we end up working ahead of schedule, we also want to look at lightweight animation and motion effects to improve how realistic the game environment is.

Butterfly Animation

We plan to create a Unity script that animates the butterfly objects by making them orbit designated trees in a circular path, which can be defined by cosine and sine functions. Additionally, we may design a flapping motion by creating and interpolating between several key frames of wing positions.

Tree Swaying

By editing the vertex shader, we can incorporate a random noise function to subtly displace tree vertices. This will simulate natural motion, creating the idea of trees gently swaying in the wind.

Resulting Demo

In summary of our above overview, the result that we are going for is a Japan-themed Minecraft mod with:

  • New Assets: butterflies, swans, mountain tops
  • Texture Effects: cherry blossoms pattern applied on vanilla trees, cobblestone pattern applied on vanilla walking paths
  • Style / Non-Photorealistic Effects: watercolor-style with soft lighting and shading, desaturation, grainy effect, stylized outlines
  • (Aspirational) Motion Effects: butterflies moving about trees and flapping wings, trees swaying
  • All effects tested and integrated into a bundled .zip shader and resource pack, ideally ready for installation via Optifine
Why are these goals accomplish-able?

We think that we can accomplish these goals because previous assignments and course material have given us plenty of groundwork. In Homework 1, we implemented texture mapping in the 2D space, as well as anti-aliasing techniques like bilinear interpolation and level sampling with mipmaps. Here, we will build on this to then match 2D texture patches to 3D solids within the Minecraft mesh and compute anti-aliasing strategies in a higher dimension.

In the Shader workshop, we learned that game engines like Unity are relatively user-friendly, with resources to easily upload templates, personalized meshes, assets, textures and scripts into the rendering pipeline. We think by choosing this pipeline, we can use these built-in tools and extend upon them, making this project both more feasible and creatively flexible.

Quality / Performance Evaluation

To measure the quality of this baseline shader system, we will create a metric to visually identify and evaluate the number of artifacts in the scene. This includes edges with jaggies and distorted textures (overly stretched, twisted, blurred) and can eventually be graphed to show successful anti-aliasing over our iterative improvements.

We can also assess our synthesized renderings against the ground truth. By measuring metrics like mean squared error (MSE) or the Structural Similarity Index Measure (SSIM), we can compute pixel-level differences in structure, color and texture. Our goal will similarly be to find regions where these errors exceed a certain threshold, then edit our scripts to reduce them accordingly.

Finally, we can benchmark performance in terms of gameplay smoothness and responsiveness. We can maintain a sufficient frame rate by comparing FPS using the Minecraft debug overlay before and after mod activation, targeting an FPS above 30. Additionally, we can measure shader switching times and visual transition delays, aiming to keep them under 100 ms.

Computing Platform, Hardware and Software

We'll primarily use our personal laptops for general coding, debugging, and development. For rendering shaders and animations, we will SSH into the EECS Instructional Machines, as they have GPUs better suited for graphics-intensive tasks.

We are most likely going to use these software resources:

  • Blender to create our new assets and export as .obj files
  • Pass .obj files to Blockbench, which will convert .obj into .json files for better compatibility with Minecraft and texture mapping
  • Unity to design and prototype the shaders
  • Finalized shaders will be coded in GLSL to fit Minecraft’s Optifine mod
